# Summary Patterns

Load this file before writing or revising a summary. A summary is optional. If it does not add a dimension the bullets cannot carry, cut it.

## 1. When to Use a Summary

Use a summary when it helps explain:

- a leadership level that is not obvious from title alone
- a pivot or narrative transition
- a cross-functional or cross-domain through-line
- a scope-of-influence story that the first few bullets will then prove

Skip it when the first role and headline already do the work.

## Hierarchy of preference

When the candidate has distinctive proof points (named employers, scale numbers, published work, regulated-domain credentials), prefer the **compressed through-line** shape over the **trait-led executive** shape. The fix for "reads like three bullets fused" is *shorter and more compressed*, not *more abstract*.

The wrong tradeoff is `5-sentence inventory` vs. `2-sentence abstract`. The right resolution is concrete + compressed:

1. **Compressed through-line (3–4 sentences, employer-anchored)** — preferred when candidate has distinctive proof points. See §2 first shape.
2. **Executive summary (2–3 sentences, trait-led)** — only when proof points are generic, employer names don't move the reader, or the candidate is making a clean pivot where prior employers don't transfer.
3. **No summary** — when headline + first role already establish the story.

Rules of thumb:

- A summary **may** name employers if they are doing real proof work (scope, regulated context, scale, publication).
- A summary **should not** become one sentence per job. If sentences 2-4 are each a different employer with no through-line linking them, compress.
- **Abstract self-description is worse than concise factual specificity.** Trait-led sentences ("My work has centered on roadmap ownership, risk control, and adoption") fail the AI-slop test even when they're shorter.
- **If the only way to make the summary short is to make it generic, cut it instead.**

## 2. Default Shapes

### Compressed through-line (preferred when candidate has distinctive proof points)

```text
[Real lane.] [One sentence threading 2-3 employer-anchored proof points into a single through-line.] [Optional current-state sentence with named differentiator.] [Optional YOE tail.]
```

Example:

AI leader focused on taking models from research to production in high-stakes environments. Built HIPAA-compliant ML at Adaptive Biotechnologies supporting published SARS-CoV-2 research, then scaled AI strategy at Govini to ship a production RAG platform for Department of Defense clients. Now at DeepLearning.AI under Andrew Ng on agentic systems and open-source tooling like claude_flow. 11 years in ML, 7 in leadership.

What this gets right:

- First sentence names a real lane (research-to-production / high-stakes).
- Second sentence is one sentence, not three — Adaptive and Govini are linked by *"then scaled... to ship"*, signaling continuity rather than parallel inventory.
- Third sentence brings the current state and a named differentiator (claude_flow).
- Employer names earn their spot — each one anchors a different scope (regulated-healthcare → DoD-enterprise → frontier).

### Executive summary (trait-led)

```text
[Real lane.] [Decision/governance/scope sentence.] [Optional differentiator sentence.]
```

Use only when proof points are generic or the candidate is mid-pivot. When the candidate has Adaptive/Govini/DLAI-tier proof points, this shape under-uses them.

Example:

AI leader focused on turning emerging model capability into production systems teams can govern and scale. My work has centered on roadmap ownership, risk control, and adoption in regulated environments where reliability matters more than novelty.

### Strategic technical leader

```text
[Lane.] [Concrete work pattern.] [Why that matters for the target role.]
```

Example:

I build production AI systems and the operating patterns around them. The through-line in my work is translating fast-moving model capability into workflows, evaluation standards, and tooling other teams can actually use.

### DeepLearning.AI transition

```text
[Past scope.] [Current leverage shift.] [What that means now.]
```

Example:

After years of leading AI systems in regulated environments, I moved into a curriculum product role at DeepLearning.AI to work closer to where new patterns in LLMs and agentic systems are emerging. The shift was from shipping one system at a time to turning repeatable patterns into tooling, frameworks, and training that help many teams build faster.

### Executive plus hands-on credibility

```text
[Direction-setting sentence.] [Hands-on credibility sentence.] [Constraint or domain sentence.]
```

Example:

I lead AI roadmap, governance, and cross-functional execution with a bias toward production reality. I still go deep enough in systems, tooling, and evaluation to make sound build-vs-buy and release decisions, especially in regulated environments.

## 3. Summary Anti-Patterns

Reject summaries that:

- read like three bullets fused together
- say "results-driven", "innovative", or "dynamic"
- list technologies instead of making a point
- repeat claims the first experience bullet already proves better

Weak:

- "Results-driven AI leader with extensive experience in strategy, execution, and innovation across cross-functional teams."

Better:

- "I lead AI work where production constraints are real and governance cannot be an afterthought."

## 4. Kill Switch

Delete the summary if:

- the headline and first role already establish the story
- the summary only restates the title in softer words
- every sentence could be replaced by a stronger first bullet
