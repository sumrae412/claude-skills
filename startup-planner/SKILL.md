# Startup Planning Workshop

A guided workshop for taking a product idea from conception through execution planning. Stages 1-7 apply Guy Kawasaki's Conception framework; Stages 8-12 add structured business frameworks for market validation, MVP definition, and investor-ready deliverables.

## Before Starting

1. Read `references/kawasaki-frameworks.md` for Kawasaki framework details
2. Read `references/business-frameworks.md` for BMC, market analysis, MVP, and pitch deck frameworks
3. Optionally read `references/project-context-template.md` to understand what product context to gather
4. Ask which stage to work on (or start from Stage 1)

## Workshop Stages

Work through ONE stage per session. Each stage produces a concrete output.

---

## Phase 1: Conception (Kawasaki Framework)

### Stage 1: Genesis Question
**Goal**: Identify which "simple question" drove the product's creation

Ask:
- "Which of these best describes how your idea started?"
  - "Therefore, what?" (spotted a trend, followed consequences)
  - "Isn't this interesting?" (curiosity/accidental discovery)
  - "Is there a better way?" (frustration with status quo)
  - "Why doesn't our company do this?" (employer frustration)
  - "It's possible, so why don't we make it?" (what-the-hell attitude)
  - "Where is the market leader weak?" (competitive gap)

Follow up: "Tell me the specific moment or realization that sparked this."

**Output**: One paragraph capturing the genesis story.

---

### Stage 2: Sweet Spot Analysis
**Goal**: Map the Expertise + Opportunity + Passion intersection

Ask about each circle:

**Expertise** (what you can build):
- "What technical skills do you bring?"
- "What domain knowledge do you have?"
- "What's missing that you'd need to hire for?"

**Opportunity** (market reality):
- "Who specifically would pay for this today?"
- "How big is the target market?"
- "What's your evidence that this opportunity exists?"

**Passion** (sustainable motivation):
- "What about this problem keeps you working on it?"
- "Could you work on this for 10 years?"

**Output**: Three bullet lists (Expertise, Opportunity, Passion) + assessment of overlap.

---

### Stage 3: Make Meaning
**Goal**: Define how the product makes the world better

Ask: "Complete this sentence: If [product] never existed, the world would be worse off because __________."

Challenge weak answers:
- "Makes people more efficient" -> Push: "So what? What's the human impact?"
- "Saves time" -> Push: "Time for what? What do they do with that time?"

Good meaning statements connect to human outcomes.

**Output**: One sentence stating the product's meaning.

---

### Stage 4: Make Mantra
**Goal**: Create a 3-4 word mantra (NOT a mission statement)

Explain the difference:
- **Mission statement** (bad): "We leverage cutting-edge AI to empower professionals with workflow automation solutions"
- **Mantra** (good): "Never drop the ball" or "Automate the follow-up"

Characteristics:
- 3-4 words max
- Positive and outward-focused
- Explains what you do for customers
- Receptionist could say it

Workshop approach:
1. List 5-10 candidate mantras
2. Apply the "receptionist test" - would they know what it means?
3. Apply the "opposite test" - does competition say the opposite?
4. Pick top 3, then narrow to 1

**Output**: Final mantra + 2-3 runners-up.

---

### Stage 5: Business Model (Kawasaki)
**Goal**: Answer "Who has your money?" and "How do you get it?"

Walk through Kawasaki's model types:
- **SaaS/Freemium**: Free tier -> paid tiers
- **Printer and Toner**: Base product + recurring revenue
- **Market Leader**: Premium pricing for best-in-class
- **Switchboard**: Control supply/marketplace
- **Eyeballs**: Advertising/sponsorship
- **Craftsman**: Highest quality, may stay small

Ask:
- "Who exactly is writing the check?"
- "What's the price point that makes this a no-brainer?"
- "Can you describe the model in 10 words or fewer?"

**Output**: Business model in <=10 words + pricing hypothesis.

---

### Stage 6: Soul Mates Assessment
**Goal**: Evaluate cofounder needs and gaps

Ask about current state:
- "Are you building this solo or with cofounders?"
- "What roles are you covering yourself?" (product, engineering, sales, marketing)

If solo, explore the gap:
- "What's the one role you most need a cofounder for?"
- "Would you hire a cofounder for fundability or because you genuinely need them?"

Kawasaki's cofounder framework:
- **Similarities needed**: Vision, Size ambitions, Commitment level
- **Differences needed**: Expertise (builder vs seller), Orientation (detail vs big picture), Perspective (diverse backgrounds)

**Output**: Current team assessment + #1 cofounder gap (if any).

---

### Stage 7: MATT (Milestones, Assumptions, Tests, Tasks)
**Goal**: Create the tactical execution framework

**Milestones** (5 most important):
1. Working prototype
2. Initial capital
3. Field-testable version
4. Paying customer
5. Cash-flow breakeven

Ask: "Where are you on each? What's the next milestone to hit?"

**Assumptions** (test these):
- Market size, Customer acquisition cost, Conversion rate, Price point acceptance, Sales cycle length, Churn rate

Ask: "Which assumption, if wrong, would kill the business?"

**Tests** (validate assumptions):
- How will you test customer acquisition cost?
- How will you test price sensitivity?
- What's your "smoke test" for demand?

**Tasks** (immediate actions):
- What must happen this week?
- What must happen this month?

**Output**: MATT document with current status on each section.

## Phase 2: Execution Planning

### Stage 8: Business Model Canvas & Value Proposition
**Goal**: Structure the business model using the BMC 9-component framework and validate the value proposition

**Part A: Value Proposition Canvas**

Build the Customer Profile:
- **Customer Jobs**: What functional, social, and emotional tasks are they trying to get done?
- **Pains**: What frustrates them? What obstacles exist? What could go wrong?
- **Gains**: What are their required, expected, desired, and unexpected gains?

Build the Value Map:
- **Products & Services**: What do you offer to help customers get jobs done?
- **Pain Relievers**: How do you eliminate or reduce their pains?
- **Gain Creators**: How do you create customer gains?

Ask: "For [target customer] who [need], our [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitor], we [differentiation]."

**Part B: Business Model Canvas**

Walk through all 9 components (see `references/business-frameworks.md` for detail):
1. Customer Segments
2. Value Propositions (from Part A)
3. Channels
4. Customer Relationships
5. Revenue Streams (refine from Stage 5)
6. Key Resources
7. Key Activities
8. Key Partnerships
9. Cost Structure

Ask for each: one sentence max. The BMC should fit on one page.

**Output**: Completed Value Proposition Canvas + one-page BMC.

---

### Stage 9: Market Analysis & Competitive Positioning
**Goal**: Size the market, analyze competition, and create customer personas

**Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)**:
- **TAM**: Total market demand if you had 100% market share
- **SAM**: Segment you can realistically target with your go-to-market
- **SOM**: What you can capture in 1-3 years

Ask:
- "What data sources can we use to estimate market size?"
- "What's your realistic first-year target?"

Use web search to validate market size claims with real data.

**Competitive Analysis**:
For each competitor (direct + indirect), assess:
- Product/service offering, pricing, positioning
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Market share and customer sentiment

Create a positioning map on 2 key dimensions. Ask: "What two axes best differentiate you?"

**Customer Personas** (2-3):
- Name, background, demographics
- Goals, pain points, buying behavior
- Decision criteria, information sources

**Red flags to watch for**:
- No clear market need
- Saturated market with strong competitors
- Shrinking market
- Unclear differentiation

**Output**: TAM/SAM/SOM estimates + competitive positioning map + 2-3 personas.

---

### Stage 10: MVP Definition & Requirements
**Goal**: Define minimum viable product scope with prioritized features and user stories

**Core Hypothesis**: Ask "What single thing must be true for this to work?"

**Feature Prioritization (MoSCoW)**:
- **Must Have**: Critical for MVP - product has no value without these
- **Should Have**: Important but not critical - add soon after MVP
- **Could Have**: Nice to have - future enhancements
- **Won't Have**: Explicitly excluded from scope

For complex prioritization, apply **RICE scoring**:
RICE = (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort

**User Stories** (for Must Have features):
- "As a [persona], I want to [action] so that [benefit]"
- Include acceptance criteria for each

**MVP Pattern** - ask which fits:
- **Concierge MVP**: Manual delivery of service first
- **Wizard of Oz MVP**: Automated frontend, manual backend
- **Single-Feature MVP**: One feature done extremely well
- **Landing Page MVP**: Test demand before building

Ask: "What's the smallest version that tests your core hypothesis?"

**Non-functional requirements**: Performance, usability, accessibility, security (basics only)

**Scope reduction techniques**: Remove features, simplify features, narrow target audience

Anti-patterns to avoid:
- Feature creep ("just one more feature")
- Perfectionism (MVP too polished)
- Kitchen sink MVP (trying to serve everyone)
- No hypothesis (building without clear validation goal)

**Output**: MVP scope document with MoSCoW features, user stories, and success metrics.

---

### Stage 11: Go/NoGo Decision
**Goal**: Make a structured decision about whether to proceed, pivot, or stop

**Review Past Decisions** (if any exist from previous sessions):
Check for prior decision records. Extract learnings: what worked, what failed, what assumptions were wrong. This cross-project learning improves decisions over time.

**Go/NoGo Checklist**:

Go criteria (must meet majority):
- Strong market validation
- Clear value proposition
- Feasible with available resources
- Aligned with business goals
- Acceptable risk level
- Competitive advantage identified
- Positive projected ROI

NoGo criteria (any one may trigger):
- Weak or no market validation
- Unclear value proposition
- Resource constraints too severe
- Unacceptable risks
- No sustainable advantage

**Risk Assessment** (2x2 matrix):
For each risk, plot Probability x Impact:
- High Prob + High Impact: Mitigate immediately
- High Prob + Low Impact: Monitor
- Low Prob + High Impact: Contingency plan
- Low Prob + Low Impact: Accept

**Bias Check** - before deciding, review for:
- Confirmation bias (seeking only confirming evidence)
- Sunk cost fallacy (continuing due to past investment)
- Optimism bias (overestimating success)

Ask: "Imagine it's 12 months from now and this failed. What was the most likely cause?" (pre-mortem)

**Decision Documentation**:
Record the decision with context, options considered, rationale, risks, success criteria, next steps, and review date.

**Output**: Go/NoGo decision document with rationale, risk register, and next steps.

---

### Stage 12: Pitch Deck
**Goal**: Create an investor-ready pitch deck from all previous outputs

Generate a **Marp-compatible markdown** slide deck (12-15 slides):

1. **Title** - Product name + tagline
2. **Problem** - Pain points with evidence
3. **Solution** - Key benefits (3 max)
4. **Market Opportunity** - TAM/SAM/SOM from Stage 9
5. **Product/MVP** - Core features from Stage 10
6. **Business Model** - Revenue streams from Stages 5 & 8
7. **Competitive Advantage** - Positioning from Stage 9
8. **Go-to-Market Strategy** - Phased approach
9. **Financial Projections** - Revenue/customers/ARR table
10. **Team** - From Stage 6 (if applicable)
11. **Milestones & Roadmap** - From Stage 7
12. **Ask/Next Steps** - What you need

**Slide rules**:
- One key message per slide
- 3-5 bullets max, 500 chars total per slide
- Data properly cited with sources

**Export**: Use Marp CLI (`marp slides.md -o slides.pdf`), VS Code Marp extension, or https://web.marp.app

See `references/business-frameworks.md` for the full Marp slide template.

**Output**: Complete Marp markdown file ready for export to PDF/PPTX.

## Session Flow

1. **Start**: "Which stage do you want to work on? Or should we start from Stage 1?"
2. **During**: Ask questions one at a time. Push back on weak answers. Never assume - always ask.
3. **End**: Summarize the output. Ask "Ready for the next stage, or want to refine this one?"

## Output Format

After completing a stage, provide a clean summary block:

```

[Output content]

Completed: [date]
Next stage: [X+1 name]
```
