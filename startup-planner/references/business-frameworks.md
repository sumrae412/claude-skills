# Business Frameworks Reference

Frameworks used in Stages 8-12 of the Startup Planning Workshop. Sourced from Business Model Canvas, Value Proposition Canvas, Lean Startup, and standard market analysis methodologies.

---

## 1. Value Proposition Canvas

### Customer Profile

**Customer Jobs**:
- Functional Jobs: Tasks customers are trying to get done
- Social Jobs: How customers want to be perceived
- Emotional Jobs: How customers want to feel

**Pains** (rate severity: moderate to extreme):
- Undesired outcomes, problems, characteristics
- Obstacles preventing job completion
- Risks (what could go wrong?)

**Gains**:
- Required gains: Minimum expectations
- Expected gains: What customers expect
- Desired gains: What would delight them
- Unexpected gains: What exceeds expectations

### Value Map

**Pain Relievers**: How your product eliminates or reduces pains (rank by importance)
**Gain Creators**: How your product creates gains (rank by relevance)

### Fit Assessment Checklist
- [ ] Clear customer segment identified
- [ ] Customer jobs well understood
- [ ] Pains and gains validated
- [ ] Products address top pains
- [ ] Products create top gains
- [ ] Differentiated from competitors

### Value Proposition Statement Template
**For** [target customer] **who** [need], **our** [product] **is a** [category] **that** [key benefit]. **Unlike** [competitor], **we** [differentiation].

### Common Value Proposition Patterns

| Pattern | Pain | Gain | Example |
|---------|------|------|---------|
| Time-saving | Process is slow/complex | Save time, simplify | "Get X done in minutes instead of hours" |
| Cost reduction | Current solution expensive | Reduce costs, improve ROI | "Cut costs by X% while maintaining quality" |
| Performance | Current solutions underperform | Better results | "Achieve X% better results" |
| Risk mitigation | High risk of failure/errors | Reduce risk, reliability | "Eliminate risk of X" |
| Accessibility | Only available to few | Available to many | "Enterprise-grade X for everyone" |

## 2. Business Model Canvas (9 Components)

### 1. Customer Segments
Who are we creating value for? Types: Mass market, Niche, Segmented, Diversified, Multi-sided

### 2. Value Propositions
What value do we deliver? Elements: Newness, Performance, Customization, Design, Brand/Status, Price, Cost reduction, Risk reduction, Accessibility, Convenience

### 3. Channels
How do we reach customers? Phases: Awareness -> Evaluation -> Purchase -> Delivery -> After-sales

### 4. Customer Relationships
What relationship does each segment expect? Types: Personal assistance, Self-service, Automated, Communities, Co-creation

### 5. Revenue Streams
For what value are customers willing to pay? Types: Asset sale, Usage fee, Subscription, Licensing, Brokerage, Advertising

### 6. Key Resources
What do our value propositions require? Types: Physical, Intellectual, Human, Financial

### 7. Key Activities
What activities are essential? Types: Production, Problem solving, Platform/network

### 8. Key Partnerships
Who are key partners/suppliers? Types: Strategic alliances, Coopetition, Joint ventures, Buyer-supplier

### 9. Cost Structure
Most important costs? Types: Cost-driven vs Value-driven. Characteristics: Fixed, Variable, Economies of scale/scope

### Common Business Model Patterns

**SaaS**: Specific segment -> Efficiency/automation -> Online/direct -> Subscription -> Platform + tech + data
**Marketplace**: Multi-sided (buyers + sellers) -> Access/convenience/trust -> Transaction fees -> Platform management
**Freemium**: Free tier + paid premium -> Self-service + upselling -> User acquisition + conversion optimization

## 3. Market Analysis

### TAM/SAM/SOM
- **TAM** (Total Addressable Market): Total market demand
- **SAM** (Serviceable Addressable Market): Segment you can target
- **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market): What you can realistically capture

### Customer Persona Template
- Name and background
- Demographics
- Goals and motivations
- Pain points and frustrations
- Buying behavior and decision criteria
- Information sources

### Competitive Analysis Template
For each competitor:
- Product/service offering
- Pricing strategy and model
- Market positioning
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Market share estimate
- Customer sentiment

### Positioning Map
Plot competitors on 2 key dimensions relevant to your market.

### Research Methods
- **Primary**: Customer interviews, surveys, user testing, direct observation
- **Secondary**: Industry reports, government stats, academic studies, competitor websites
- **Online**: Industry analysis, competitor research, market trends, customer reviews

### Red Flags
- No clear market need
- Saturated market with strong competitors
- Shrinking market
- High barriers to entry
- Unclear differentiation
- Weak validation

## 4. MVP Definition

### MoSCoW Prioritization
- **Must Have**: Critical for MVP, directly delivers core value
- **Should Have**: Important, enhance experience, add post-MVP
- **Could Have**: Desirable, low priority, future
- **Won't Have**: Explicitly excluded

### RICE Scoring
RICE = (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort
- **Reach**: Users affected per time period
- **Impact**: Minimal=0.25, Low=0.5, Medium=1, High=2, Massive=3
- **Confidence**: Low=50%, Medium=80%, High=100%
- **Effort**: Person-months

### MVP Patterns
- **Concierge**: Manual delivery, validate demand before automation
- **Wizard of Oz**: Automated frontend, manual backend
- **Single-Feature**: One feature done extremely well
- **Landing Page**: Measure interest via sign-ups before building

### Scope Reduction Techniques
- Remove features (question every one)
- Simplify features (manual processes initially)
- Narrow target (smaller segment, specific use case)

### Anti-Patterns
- Feature creep, Perfectionism, Wrong priorities, Scope too large, No hypothesis, Kitchen sink MVP

## 5. Decision Framework

### Go/NoGo Criteria

**Go** (must meet majority):
- Strong market validation
- Clear value proposition
- Feasible with available resources
- Aligned with business goals
- Acceptable risk level
- Competitive advantage identified
- Positive projected ROI

**NoGo** (any one may trigger):
- Weak/no market validation
- Unclear value proposition
- Resource constraints too severe
- Unacceptable risks
- No sustainable advantage

### Risk Assessment Matrix

| | Low Impact | High Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **High Probability** | Monitor | Mitigate Immediately |
| **Low Probability** | Accept | Contingency Plan |

Risk types: Technical, Market, Financial, Operational, Competitive, Legal/regulatory

### Cognitive Bias Checklist
- Confirmation bias: Seeking only confirming evidence
- Anchoring: Over-relying on first information
- Sunk cost fallacy: Continuing due to past investment
- Optimism bias: Overestimating success probability
- Groupthink: Conformity over critical thinking

Mitigation: Seek disconfirming evidence, use structured frameworks, get diverse perspectives, run pre-mortems.

### Decision Documentation Template
```
# Decision: [Title]
## Context: [Background]
## Options Considered: [List with pros/cons]
## Decision: [What was decided]
## Rationale: [Why]
## Risks & Mitigation: [List]
## Success Criteria: [How to measure]
## Next Steps: [Actions with owners and dates]
## Review Date: [When to revisit]
```

### Two-Way vs One-Way Doors
- **Two-way door** (reversible): Decide quickly, bias toward action, iterate
- **One-way door** (irreversible): Take time, gather comprehensive data, use rigorous framework
