# Pitch Deck Content Guide

Based on YC's frameworks for what investors need to hear and remember.

---

## The Vertebrae Method

Before writing any slides, identify your 3–4 vertebrae — the most compelling, memorable facts about your
company. These are not necessarily what explains what you do; they're what makes investors afraid to miss
the deal.

Derive them by asking:
- What are we building and for whom?
- Why hasn't this been done before?
- Why is this genuinely hard to do?
- Why is this an opportunity not to be missed?

Every slide should serve at least one of these vertebrae. If a slide doesn't connect to any of them, cut it.

**Important:** Lead with your strongest vertebra. If you have $1M ARR growing 3x YoY, that is slide one —
not slide five. Don't bury the lead.

## Slide-by-Slide Content Guide

### 1. Cover

**One job:** Tell investors exactly what you do and make the traction undeniable before you say a word.

**Format:**
- Company name in massive type — the whole left half
- One-line tagline in small gray text below the name
- 3 key stats in white cards on the right (ARR, growth rate, customer count)
- Raise amount in small monospace at the bottom left

**Rules:**
- The tagline is one sentence. Subject + verb + object. No jargon.
- Stats on the cover should be your 3 strongest numbers — the ones that make investors lean forward
- Do not save the reveal for later — state what you do immediately

**Good tagline:** "Automated bookkeeping for independent restaurants."
**Bad tagline:** "Next-generation AI-powered financial intelligence for the food ecosystem."

---

### 2. Problem

**One job:** Make investors feel the pain before you show the solution.

**Format:** Big bold headline left (3–5 words, the gut-punch statement). Numbered white cards right (01–04) naming each specific pain point in monospace caps.

**Rules:**
- The headline is the problem in its most dramatic, honest form
- Each numbered card is a distinct symptom, not an explanation — the name alone should land
- Quantify in the headline or subtext where possible: "$500–$1,500/mo for the wrong tool"
- Small supporting text below the headline (1–2 sentences max) for context
- Do NOT spend so long on the problem that the audience forgets you have a solution

**The test:** After the problem slide, does the audience feel urgency? If not, the problem isn't specific or painful enough.

---

### 3. Product

**One job:** Explain clearly what you built and exactly who uses it.

**Format:** Big bold headline left ("Clean P&L. No effort." style — outcome, not feature). 2×2 grid of white cards right showing the numbered workflow steps (01 Connect, 02 Sync, 03 Categorize, 04 Deliver).

**Rules:**
- The headline states the outcome for the customer, not a feature description
- Each card: number in accent blue, step name in monospace bold, one-line description in gray
- No screenshots. Ever. Describe the product as a workflow.
- Be specific about the customer in the subtext
- Price point goes in small monospace at the bottom

**Good format:**
```
01 CONNECT   — POS · payroll · invoices
02 SYNC      — every transaction, daily
03 CATEGORIZE — COGS · tips · labor
04 DELIVER   — clean P&L on the 1st
```

---

### 4. Market (Opportunity)

**One job:** Convince investors the prize is worth chasing.

**Format:** Big bold headline left (the TAM conclusion, stated directly: "$180M ARR. US alone."). Right side: two stacked white cards with the key numbers (total addressable count, addressable ARR). Bottom left: a "THE MATH" card showing the bottoms-up calculation in monospace.

**Rules:**
- Show the math — even simple math signals you've thought it through
- Bottoms-up only: [# customers] × [% addressable] × [ACV] = TAM
- Never cite a Gartner report as your primary market evidence
- If the market seems small, explain the expansion path in the subtext

**Bottoms-up (good):**
> "500,000 restaurants × 10% × $3,588/yr = $180M"

**Top-down (bad):**
> "The restaurant industry is a $1 trillion market. If we capture 1%..."

---

### 5. Traction

**One job:** Show that the story is non-fiction.

**Format:** Big bold headline left (the growth stat stated as fact: "18% MoM. 4 months."). Line chart below the headline. Two or three white stat cards on the right (ARR, customer count, notable customers).

**Rules:**
- Write the insight AS the headline — never make investors read the chart and calculate growth themselves
- Use month-over-month line chart, not cumulative. Cumulative graphs always look like growth and trigger suspicion.
- Label data points on the chart
- Notable customers go in the third right-side card — name-dropping signals legitimacy
- If pre-traction: lead with team credibility + LOIs/pilots + customer discovery quotes

**Metrics hierarchy (use your strongest):**
1. Revenue / ARR (best)
2. Paying customers / MRR growth
3. Active users with strong retention
4. Waitlist or LOIs (if pre-launch)
5. Pilot agreements with named companies

---

### 6. Team

**One job:** Make investors believe YOU are the team that will win this market.

**Format:** Big bold headline ("Both sides of the problem." style — the unfair advantage in one phrase). Two white cards side by side — one per founder. Each card: role tag (CEO/CTO), credential in monospace bold, one-sentence "why it matters" in gray.

**Rules:**
- Only include credentials directly relevant to why you'll win THIS market
- One credential per person maximum — the most relevant one, stated crisply
- The "why it matters" line connects their background to the specific problem
- Don't list full work history. Don't say "passionate about this problem."

**Strong credentials:**
- "6 yrs GM & operator, 4-location restaurant group"
- "Built data pipeline infra at Toast"

**Weak credentials:**
- "10+ years of experience in tech"
- "MBA from [school]"

---

### 7. Ask / Conclusion

**One job:** Make sure investors leave remembering your vertebrae and know exactly what you're raising.

**Format:** Big bold headline is the raise amount ("$1.5M pre-seed."). Four numbered white cards below — each one restates a vertebra as a crisp fact + context pair. Email at the bottom left.

**Rules:**
- The raise amount IS the headline — state it immediately
- Each numbered card: accent-blue bold label on left (the vertebra in 2–3 words), gray description on right
- Restate your vertebrae — don't introduce new ideas
- Include a clear milestone for the raise: "Target: $500K ARR before seed"
- End with contact info, not a thank-you

**Good conclusion structure:**
```
01  $143K ARR       18% MoM, 4 months straight
02  UNIQUE TEAM     Operated restaurants + built Toast infra
03  500K MARKET     No tools built for independent restaurants
04  TARGET          $500K ARR before seed
```

## Business Model

No dedicated slide is always required, but investors must understand the model by the end of the deck.
Weave it in naturally — pricing goes on the Product slide (small monospace at the bottom). Unit economics
must be plausible. If your model requires massive scale, acknowledge that and explain the path.

---

## Story Architecture

The whole deck is a story. Structure it to build momentum:

```
Hook (Cover)
  → Tension (Problem)
    → Resolution (Product)
      → Scale (Market)
        → Proof (Traction)
          → Trust (Team)
            → Ask (Conclusion)
```

If you have extraordinary traction, lead with it. The structure above is default, not mandatory. Always
ask: "What's the most compelling fact about this company?" — then figure out how to get there as fast as possible.
