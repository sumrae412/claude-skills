# Communication Principles

Shared reference for any skill that produces writing, speech, or other author-to-audience communication. Linked from `resume-tailor`, `sc-marketing-scripts`, and other writing skills.

The reference is short on purpose. It names the principles and shows the failure modes; the host skill decides how to apply them.

---

## 1. Audience-centered focus

Ask **"what does the audience need to know?"** — not **"what do I want to say?"**

- The author's notes, research trail, and drafting process do not belong in the final artifact.
- Cut anything that serves the author's ego, completeness anxiety, or fear of being misread.
- If a sentence exists to prove the author did the work, cut it. The work shows in the result.

## 2. Simplicity

Use the simplest word that carries the meaning. Short sentences. If a word can be cut, cut it.

- Plain verbs over Latinate nouns ("use" not "utilization," "help" not "facilitation").
- One idea per sentence. Break compound sentences that force re-reading.
- Remove hedges and intensifiers that add no information ("really," "very," "actually," "just," "basically").

## 3. Lead with the conclusion

Start with the recommendation, verdict, or key point. Respect the reader's time.

- Emails: the ask or outcome in the first sentence, context after.
- Presentations and memos: the headline finding before the supporting data.
- Resumes and bios: the strongest evidence first — do not bury it under chronology.

## 4. Writing vs. speaking: calibrate to the medium

- **Email:** concise. A single request, clear subject, no more than the reader needs.
- **Presentation / script / voiceover:** tell a story. Data supports the arc; it is not the arc.
- **Document:** scan-friendly. Headings, short paragraphs, a reader should find their section without reading everything.

## 5. Active listening

Great communicators listen more than they talk. In advisory or interview-style skills:

- Ask, then wait. Do not fill silence with the next prompt.
- Reflect back what you heard before pivoting — it surfaces misreads early.
- Listen for what the person *didn't* say; the gap is often the real constraint.

## 6. "I help" framing for introductions

In networking, bios, summaries, or opening lines: explain what you do by **how you help others** — not by job title or output.

- ❌ "I'm a senior backend engineer working on distributed systems."
- ✅ "I help teams keep their data pipelines reliable when traffic spikes."

The help-frame is specific, memorable, and gives the reader a handle to keep talking.

## 7. Preparation

Effective communication is a skill that requires **deliberate preparation** — not improvisation, not winging it.

- For any significant message: draft, cut, re-read, cut again.
- Know the audience, the ask, and the one thing you want them to remember before writing the first sentence.
- If preparation reveals you don't have a clear point, stop. No amount of polish rescues an unclear message.

## 8. Control your message

You control three things: **yourself, your message, and your interaction with the audience.** You do not control how the audience feels, what they do with the message, or what they already believe when they encounter it.

- Write to what you control. Do not try to pre-empt every possible misreading.
- Hold the line on clarity, honesty, and calibrated confidence. Do not soften into vagueness to avoid imagined objections.
- If the audience pushes back, listen (principle 5). Do not apologize for the message itself unless it was actually wrong.

---

## Quick checklist

Before shipping any piece of writing produced under a skill that loads this reference:

- [ ] Starts with the conclusion / recommendation / hook — not setup.
- [ ] Every sentence earns its place. Cut words until cuts hurt the meaning.
- [ ] Written in plain language — no jargon the audience hasn't earned.
- [ ] Tuned to the medium (email vs. script vs. doc vs. resume bullet).
- [ ] Audience could state the one key takeaway in their own words after reading.
- [ ] No ego residue, no completeness-anxiety filler, no hedges that blur the claim.
