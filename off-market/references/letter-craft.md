# Letter Craft

A letter from this skill is not a marketing piece. It is a note from a real person to another real person about a specific house on a specific street. Read this guide before drafting and after — the second pass is the one that catches investor-jargon drift.

## Tone

- **Warm, not salesy.** You are writing to a neighbor (or a future neighbor). The voice is the same one you would use leaving a note in someone's mailbox saying "your dahlias are gorgeous, how do you keep them blooming so late?"
- **Specific to the property.** Name something real about the house, the block, the trees, the porch, the view. If the only specific thing in the letter is the street address, the letter has failed.
- **Patient, not urgent.** The recipient is not "a lead." They live there. If they decide to sell in five years, that's fine. No deadlines, no scarcity, no "I'm only in the area this week."
- **Signed by a human.** First and last name. Personal phone or email — not a 1-800 number, not a generic intake form. A real signature line beats a printed company logo every time.

## Banned phrases (investor jargon — never use)

- "We buy ugly houses"
- "Cash offer"
- "Investment opportunity"
- "Distressed property"
- "Quick close" / "fast cash"
- "As-is, no inspection"
- "Off-market deal"
- "Motivated seller"
- "Investor here"
- "I have buyers ready"
- Any reference to the recipient's situation that implies you researched their financial / legal status (no "I noticed your taxes" / "I saw the auction notice" — the letter is about the *house*, not their predicament)

## Required ingredients

Every letter should have at least one of each:

1. **A concrete observation about the house or block.** "The bay window on the second floor catches the morning light beautifully." "I love how the maple in the front yard turns red before any other tree on the street."
2. **A real reason this house, not just any house.** "We've been looking on this block for two years." "My grandmother lived three doors down on Hampton in the 1970s." "Our daughter starts kindergarten at the school at the end of the street next fall."
3. **A no-pressure invitation.** "If you ever think about selling — this year, next year, ten years from now — I'd love a chance to talk first." Not: "Call me by Friday."
4. **A real signature.** First name, last name, phone OR email (pick one, both feels like a sales contact form).

## Sample openings (use as inspiration, not templates)

> "I've walked past your house on Hampton Street every morning for the last six months on my way to work, and I keep thinking the same thing — that's the house."

> "My family and I moved to Squirrel Hill a year ago, and we've been quietly hoping one of the houses on your block might come up. Yours, with the brick porch and the old maple, is the one we keep coming back to."

> "I know this is an unusual letter to get in the mail. I'm not a real estate agent — I'm a person who has been driving down Beechwood looking for a house we'd want to raise kids in, and yours stopped me."

## Sample letter (~150 words)

> Dear neighbor on Hampton Street,
>
> I know it's unusual to get a letter like this. I'm not in real estate — I'm just someone who's been walking the East End for the last year looking for a house my family could grow into, and yours is the one that keeps stopping me.
>
> I love the bay window on the second floor, the way the porch steps are worn smooth from decades of feet, and the maple out front that goes red before anything else on the block. It's the kind of house we picture being in for thirty years.
>
> If you ever think about selling — this fall, next year, or whenever the right moment shows up — I'd love a chance to talk before anything else happens with the house. No pressure, no agents, no timeline from my end.
>
> Either way, thanks for taking care of a beautiful home.
>
> — <Your Name>
> <your-email>

## Length

- 200–400 words is the sweet spot. Under 150 feels curt; over 500 feels like a pitch deck.
- One sheet of paper. Hand-addressed envelope. First-class stamp, not metered.

## Self-check before sending

- [ ] No banned phrase appears anywhere in the letter.
- [ ] At least one concrete, property-specific observation.
- [ ] No reference to the recipient's financial / legal situation.
- [ ] Real human signature (first + last name).
- [ ] No deadline / urgency / scarcity language.
- [ ] If you swapped the address out, the letter would no longer make sense — it's not a mail merge.
- [ ] *Self-check:* the letter says nothing inferring the **recipient's** family composition, age, or household — even framing-by-omission. Writer-side family framing ("our family is looking") is fine; recipient-side ("your kids must be grown") is not.
