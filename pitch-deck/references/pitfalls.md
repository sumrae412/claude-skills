# Common Pitfalls and Fixes

## Content

### Information overload
- *Symptom:* slides crammed with detail; reader can't find the takeaway
- *Fix:* one main idea per slide. Move detail to appendix. Leave white space.
- *Best practice:* if a slide needs more than 30 words, it needs to be two
  slides — or one slide and an appendix entry.

### Weak problem statement
- *Symptom:* abstract pain, no buyer recognition, "communication gap" framing
- *Fix:* lead with concrete examples or customer quotes. The buyer should
  recognize themselves within 5 seconds of seeing the problem slide.
- *Best practice:* lead with emotional impact, follow with data — not the
  reverse.

### Missing differentiation
- *Symptom:* deck doesn't answer "why you, why not them"
- *Fix:* explicit comparison slide with your unique axis named. Don't say
  "we're better." Name the axis ("we're calendar-native; they're form-native")
  and show why it matters to the buyer.
- *Best practice:* focus on defensible moats and structural barriers, not
  feature-list parity.

### Feature dump
- *Symptom:* product slide is a bullet list of features
- *Fix:* lead with the user outcome, then 2–3 features that prove it.
  - *Bad:* "AI-powered, multi-tenant, REST API, webhooks, SSO."
  - *Good:* "Landlords go from new tenant to signed lease in 5 minutes."

### Unrealistic projections
- *Symptom:* hockey-stick growth with no underlying logic
- *Fix:* show the unit economics behind the line. CAC, payback, retention
  cohorts. If you can't show the inputs, the projection is unfalsifiable
  and reads as fiction.

### Buzzword soup
- *Symptom:* "AI-powered platform that disrupts the future of work"
- *Fix:* concrete claims only. If a phrase could appear in any deck in the
  category, cut it. Replace with what your product actually does.

### Founder ego residue
- *Symptom:* slides about "our journey," "our values," "why we started"
- *Fix:* the deck is for the audience, not the founder. Personal story
  belongs in the team slide (one line) or angel-pitch opener — not as
  filler in a VC deck.

## Design

### Inconsistent layout
- *Symptom:* font sizes, colors, alignment vary slide-to-slide
- *Fix:* one master template, applied to every slide. Reuse the same
  headline-position, body-position, footer.

### Visual without function
- *Symptom:* stock photos that decorate without arguing
- *Fix:* every visual must reinforce the headline. If a visual were removed,
  the slide should be measurably weaker.

### Walls of text
- *Symptom:* paragraph-style slides
- *Fix:* enforce 6×6 (max 6 bullets, 6 words each) or 30-words-per-slide.

### Charts without takeaways
- *Symptom:* a bar chart with no annotation, leaving the reader to infer
- *Fix:* every chart's headline names the takeaway. The chart proves it.
  - *Bad:* headline "Monthly Revenue", chart shows revenue
  - *Good:* headline "Revenue tripled in Q3 after pricing change", chart
    shows revenue with the inflection point annotated

## Delivery

### Disconnected slides
- *Symptom:* slides are individually fine but the deck has no story arc
- *Fix:* read all headlines in order. They should tell the story alone.
  If they don't, your transitions are missing.

### Weak or missing ask
- *Symptom:* deck ends without telling the audience what to do
- *Fix:* final slide names the ask, the amount/scope, and the next concrete
  step (meeting time, signed term sheet, beta access).

### Q&A not prepared
- *Symptom:* audience questions derail the pitch
- *Fix:* identify the top 5 likely questions before delivery. Prepare a
  one-sentence answer for each. Have an appendix slide for the top 3
  numbers questions (CAC, churn, headcount, runway).

### Speaking the slide aloud
- *Symptom:* presenter reads bullets verbatim while audience reads them too
- *Fix:* speaker notes contain what to *say*. Slide contains what to *show*.
  Two different pieces of content — never identical.

## Anti-patterns specific to AI-product decks

### "AI-powered" as the differentiation
- *Symptom:* the deck's wedge is "we use AI"
- *Fix:* AI is table-stakes in 2026. Differentiate on what the AI *does*
  that competitors' AI doesn't — execution depth, action breadth,
  human-in-the-loop discipline, data moat.

### No mention of failure modes
- *Symptom:* AI is described as if it's deterministic
- *Fix:* sophisticated audiences will ask about hallucination, fallback
  behavior, human-in-the-loop. Address these proactively in a
  "trust mechanics" slide or in the speaker notes.

### Inference cost ignored
- *Symptom:* unit economics slide treats LLM cost as zero
- *Fix:* show COGS line for AI inference. Investors who've seen AI startups
  fail on token economics will probe this. Pre-empting it builds credibility.
